What is Animation?

Understanding the basic concept of animation

Animation is the art and technique of creating the illusion of movement by showing a series of still images, drawings, or objects in rapid sequence. Each image is slightly different from the previous one, which makes the objects appear to move.

In simple words, animation means bringing still images to life. When many images are shown quickly (usually 24 frames per second), our brain perceives them as motion.

How Animation Works

Animation works on a principle called Persistence of Vision. Our eyes retain an image for a fraction of a second, so when multiple images are shown continuously, they blend into smooth movement.

Examples of Animation
  • Cartoon Animation: Cartoons like Tom & Jerry are created by drawing characters in different positions for each frame.
  • Animated Movies: Movies like Toy Story and Frozen use computer-generated 3D animation.
  • Flip Book Example: Drawing a stick figure on multiple pages and flipping them quickly creates animation.
Uses of Animation

Animation is widely used in cartoons, animated movies, video games, advertisements, educational videos, and visual effects in films.


12 Principles of Animation

The foundation of every great movement.

Principle 01

Squash and Stretch

The most important principle. It gives objects a sense of weight and flexibility. When a ball hits the ground, it "squashes" (flattens) to show impact; as it bounces up, it "stretches" (elongates) to show speed. Note: The total volume of the object must remain constant. If it gets wider, it must get shorter!



Principle 02

Anticipation

A small movement that prepares the audience for a bigger action. For example, a character crouches down (anticipation) before jumping into the air. Without this, movements feel sudden and robotic.



Principle 03

Staging

Staging presents an idea clearly. Proper camera angle, composition, and focus ensure the audience understands the main action.



Principle 04

Straight Ahead & Pose to Pose

Straight Ahead means drawing frame by frame, while Pose to Pose focuses on key poses first. Both methods are used depending on animation style.



Principle 05

Follow Through & Overlapping Action

These principles show that parts of a body continue moving after the main action stops, making motion more natural.



Principle 06

Slow In & Slow Out

In the real world, objects need time to accelerate and decelerate. Animators add more drawings at the beginning and end of an action to create a "slow start" and a "slow finish," making the movement feel natural.



Principle 07

Arcs

Most natural movements follow curved paths called arcs. This principle makes animation more organic.



Principle 08

Secondary Action

An extra gesture that supports the main action to add personality. If a character is walking (primary), they might be whistling or adjusting their glasses (secondary).



Principle 09

Timing

The number of frames used for a movement. More frames make the action slow and heavy; fewer frames make it fast and snappy. Timing defines the "weight" of an object.



Principle 10

Exaggeration

Exaggeration enhances actions to make them more appealing and entertaining without losing realism.



Principle 11

Solid Drawing

Solid drawing focuses on volume, weight, balance, and anatomy to give characters believable form.



Principle 12

Appeal

Appeal makes characters interesting and engaging. Good design and personality attract viewers.

Topics Covered

2D Animation

2D animation creates the illusion of movement in a flat, two-dimensional space (height and width) by sequencing individual drawings or digital frames.
3D Animation

3D animation is the process of creating moving, three-dimensional images in a digital context.
Stop Motion

Stop motion is an animation technique where physical objects are photographed in small increments, with slight adjustments between each frame.
VFX

VFX (Visual Effects) refers to imagery created or modified digitally for films, TV, and games.
